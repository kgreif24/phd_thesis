This chapter provides a very high level overview of quantum chromodynamics (QCD), with a focus on the aspects of the theory relevant to the Large Hadron Collider (LHC).
It does not survey the Standard Model or QCD with any completeness.
Instead the goal is to motivate two of the observables measured in Chapter~\ref{ch:zjets} from first principles.
They will be introduced in Sections~\ref{sec:ecc} and~\ref{sec:id}.

QCD is our current best theory of the strong nuclear force.
It affects the dynamics of all particles that carry color charge.
Another way of putting this is that they transform under a non-trivial representation of the $\mathrm{SU}(3)_c$ sub-group of the Standard Model gauge group:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{SU}(3)_c \times \mathrm{SU}(2)_L \times \mathrm{U}(1)_Y
  \label{eqn:sm_gauge_group}
\end{equation}
The particles of the Standard Model are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:sm-particles}.
The representations of each of the fundamental fields of the Standard Model (grouped as quarks, leptons, gauge bosons, and the Higgs doublet fields) are shown in Table~\ref{tab:sm-reps}.
Notably all of the fundamental fields transform in the singlet representation of $\mathrm{SU}(3)_c$ except for the quarks and gluons.
These are the particles that carry color charge and participate in strong force dynamics.
The quarks are the massive particles that make up all of the baryonic matter in the universe, and the gluons are the massless gauge bosons that mediate the strong force.
These particles are also the input particles to all LHC collisions.
The LHC is a hadron collider, meaning it collides color neutral bound states of quarks and gluons.
Most notably, the measurement in Chapter~\ref{ch:zjets} is of a cross section measured in proton--proton collisions at $\sqrt{s} = 13$~TeV.
Almost all of the LHC collisions recorded by the ATLAS detector contain strong dynamics, making grappling with the complexities of this force a central challenge of LHC physics.
In particular, the challenge is handling \textit{jets}, which are the experimental signature of the production of high transverse momentum (\pt) color-charged particles.
Jets will be introduced in Section~\ref{sec:jets}, and two methods for studying them will be introduced in Sections~\ref{sec:eec} and~\ref{sec:id}.

\subsection{The QCD Lagrangian and Asymptotic Freedom}

The dynamics of quarks and gluons are governed by the QCD Lagrangian,
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{QCD}} = -\frac{1}{4} G^a_{\mu\nu} G^{\mu\nu}_a + \sum_f \bar{q}_f \left( i \gamma^\mu D_\mu - m_f \right) q_f,
  \label{eqn:qcd_lagrangian}
\end{equation}
where the sum runs over quark flavors $f$, $D_\mu = \partial_\mu - i g_s T^a A^a_\mu$ is the covariant derivative, $g_s$ is the strong coupling constant, and $T^a$ are the generators of $\mathrm{SU}(3)$ in the fundamental representation. The gluon field strength tensor is
\begin{equation}
  G^a_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu A^a_\nu - \partial_\nu A^a_\mu + g_s f^{abc} A^b_\mu A^c_\nu,
  \label{eqn:field_strength}
\end{equation}
where $f^{abc}$ are the $\mathrm{SU}(3)$ structure constants and $A^a_\mu$ are the gluon fields.

A critical distinction between QCD and QED is that the $f^{abc} A^b_\mu A^c_\nu$ term in Equation~\ref{eqn:field_strength} is non-zero: QCD is a non-Abelian gauge theory. This term generates cubic and quartic gluon self-interaction vertices in the Feynman rules, allowing gluons to couple directly to each other, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:gluon-vertices}. In QED, the photon carries no electric charge and no analogous photon self-coupling exists.

The self-coupling of gluons has a profound consequence for the running of the strong coupling constant $\alpha_s = g_s^2 / 4\pi$. In QED, the one-loop beta function is positive, meaning the coupling increases with energy as virtual fermion loops screen the bare charge. In QCD, the gluon self-interaction contributes a large negative term to the one-loop beta function,
\begin{equation}
  \mu \frac{d\alpha_s}{d\mu} = -\frac{\alpha_s^2}{2\pi} \left( \frac{11}{3} C_A - \frac{4}{3} T_F n_f \right) + \mathcal{O}(\alpha_s^3),
  \label{eqn:beta_function}
\end{equation}
where $C_A = 3$ is the adjoint Casimir, $T_F = 1/2$, and $n_f$ is the number of active quark flavors. For $n_f \leq 16$ the coefficient is negative, so $\alpha_s$ decreases as the energy scale $\mu$ increases. This is \textit{asymptotic freedom} \cite{Gross:1973id, Politzer:1973fx}. At high energies, the strong coupling becomes weak and quarks and gluons propagate nearly freely; perturbation theory in $\alpha_s$ is reliable. At lower energies, $\alpha_s$ grows large and perturbation theory breaks down. Below the scale $\Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}} \sim 200$~MeV, the strong force becomes so powerful that color-charged particles cannot propagate freely and instead \textit{hadronize} into color-neutral bound states: the mesons and baryons that make up all baryonic matter.

\subsection{Parton Showers and Infrared Divergences}

Even at the high energies accessible to the LHC, where $\alpha_s$ is small and perturbation theory is valid, the production of a quark or gluon does not proceed quietly. The QCD splitting functions, which describe the probability of a parton undergoing a collinear splitting, contain divergences that qualitatively change the picture. The leading-order splitting functions for the three relevant partonic processes are
\begin{align}
  P_{q \to qg}(z) &= C_F \frac{1 + z^2}{1 - z}, \label{eqn:split_qqg} \\
  P_{g \to gg}(z) &= C_A \frac{(1-z(1-z))^2}{z(1-z)}, \label{eqn:split_ggg} \\
  P_{g \to q\bar{q}}(z) &= T_F \left[ z^2 + (1-z)^2 \right], \label{eqn:split_gqq}
\end{align}
where $z$ is the momentum fraction carried by one of the daughter partons, $C_F = 4/3$ is the fundamental Casimir of $\mathrm{SU}(3)$, $C_A = 3$ is the adjoint Casimir, and $T_F = 1/2$. The quark and gluon splitting functions $P_{q \to qg}$ and $P_{g \to gg}$ both diverge as $z \to 0$ or $z \to 1$, corresponding to the emission of a soft gluon (infrared divergence), and separately diverge as the emission angle $\theta \to 0$ at fixed $z$, corresponding to collinear emission. The gluon-to-quark splitting $P_{g \to q\bar{q}}$ is infrared safe but still diverges collinearly. Both the soft and collinear limits reflect the fact that the probability of emitting a soft or collinear parton is very large. Summing these emissions over a wide range of energy scales generates large logarithms of the ratio of scales that spoil the naive perturbative expansion, requiring resummation techniques to obtain reliable predictions \cite{Ellis:1991qj}.

The practical consequence is that any quark or gluon produced in an LHC collision immediately undergoes a \textit{parton shower}: a cascade of successive splittings that continues until the emitted partons reach energies near $\Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}}$, at which point they hadronize. The collimation of this shower (the reason all the resulting hadrons travel in roughly the same direction) follows directly from the collinear divergence of the splitting functions. Emissions at large angles are heavily suppressed.

\subsection{Jets}

These two sequential processes, parton showering at high energies and hadronization at low energies, together produce \textit{jets}: collimated sprays of hadrons observed in the detector. A jet is the experimental signature of a high-\pt color-charged particle produced in a hard scattering. Because the showering and hadronization processes are collinear-dominated, the energy flow of the jet is concentrated in a narrow cone around the direction of the original parton, making jets identifiable as localized deposits of energy in the calorimeter.

Jets are central objects of study in this thesis. Because they are composed of many hadrons, each individually measured by the detector, jets are high-dimensional, structured objects. This complexity makes them natural applications for machine learning, and deep learning in particular has played an increasingly important role in jet physics over the past decade \cite{Larkoski:2017jix, Kogler:2018hem}.

Much of the modern interest in jets centers not on the jet as a whole, treated as a single proxy for the initiating parton, but on its internal radiation pattern: \textit{jet substructure}. The program of jet substructure studies is organized around substructure observables, for which theoretical predictions can be computed in QCD through two complementary approaches.

The first approach is resummed perturbative QCD. A complete calculation involves three ingredients: (1) a fixed-order calculation in $\alpha_s$ capturing hard emissions, (2) resummation of the large logarithms arising from soft and collinear emissions, and (3) modeling of non-perturbative effects, typically through a shape function that accounts for hadronization corrections. The result is a systematically improvable prediction for the observable's cross section \cite{Dasgupta:2001sh}.

The second approach is Monte Carlo event generation via parton showers. A parton shower generator recursively applies splitting kernels to produce a fully exclusive partonic final state, which is then passed through a phenomenological hadronization model \cite{Marchesini:1991ch, Sjostrand:2014zea}. This approach is fully general-purpose: it can provide predictions for any observable, including those that are too complicated for analytic resummation. The limitation is that the accuracy of parton shower predictions is harder to characterize systematically, and assigned theory uncertainties are less rigorous than those from resummed calculations.

An important practical distinction between these two approaches is the form of their output. Resummed calculations provide smooth analytic predictions for inclusive cross sections; parton showers produce discrete lists of outgoing hadron four-momenta. Only the latter can be passed through a detector simulation. This difference is a subtle but important motivation for the unfolding methods discussed later in this thesis.
