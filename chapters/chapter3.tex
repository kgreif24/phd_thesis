\section{Overview: ML as a Paradigm Shift}
\label{sec:ml-overview}

Classical software is built on explicit rules: a human expert encodes domain knowledge as a sequence of instructions, and a computer evaluates those instructions on an input to produce an output.
This approach works well when the rules are known and can be stated precisely.
It fails, however, when the rules are either too complex to write down or not known at all.
Machine learning addresses this failure mode by inverting the paradigm: rather than specifying the rules, one specifies the desired input-output relationship through a dataset of examples and then uses optimization to find a function that approximates it.
The power of this shift becomes most apparent in domains where the relevant structure is real but inscrutable to human inspection.
Identifying a boosted top quark in a busy hadron collider environment is a canonical example: no human expert can write down the decision boundary in the high-dimensional space of jet constituent momenta, but a neural network trained on simulated signal and background events can learn it directly from data.

The space of machine learning methods is broad, and it is useful to organize it by the structure of the available training information.
In \textit{supervised learning}, each training example consists of an input paired with a label, and the objective is to learn a function that maps inputs to labels.
Classification and regression are the most common supervised tasks.
In \textit{unsupervised learning}, no labels are provided, and the goal is to learn useful structure in the input distribution itself, for example through clustering or dimensionality reduction.
\textit{Generative modeling} is a closely related setting in which the explicit objective is to learn the probability distribution $p(x)$ over inputs well enough to draw new samples from it or evaluate likelihoods.
Finally, \textit{reinforcement learning} (RL) optimizes the behavior of an agent interacting with an environment according to a reward signal.
RL is responsible for landmark results including AlphaGo~\cite{Silver:2016alphago} and the post-training of modern large language models~\cite{Ouyang:2022instructgpt}.
Its direct applications within HEP remain limited, though its broader trajectory is relevant to the discussion of agentic AI in Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion}.
Each of these paradigms appears in the research chapters that follow, though supervised learning and generative modeling receive the most attention.

High-energy physics presents an unusually favorable setting for machine learning, for several reasons.
The first is the availability of large, automatically labeled training datasets.
Monte Carlo event generators such as \textsc{Pythia}~\cite{Sjostrand:2014zea}, \textsc{Herwig}~\cite{Bellm:2015jjp}, and \textsc{Sherpa}~\cite{Gleisberg:2008ta} simulate the full chain of a particle collision, from the hard scatter through parton showering and hadronization to detector response.
Each simulated event carries a ground-truth label by construction, and generating many millions of such events is routine.
This is in sharp contrast to fields like medical imaging, where labeling is expensive and human-bottlenecked.
The labeling cost that limited early computer vision datasets simply does not exist in the same way in HEP.

The second reason is that HEP data has a natural high-dimensional, structured representation that plays well with modern neural network architectures.
A jet can be represented as an unordered set of constituent four-momenta, an image in the $(\eta, \phi)$ plane, or a graph with particles as nodes and pairwise relationships as edges.
These representations are natively handled by graph neural networks (GNNs) and transformers, respectively.
Furthermore, the known symmetries of the data can be built directly into the network architecture as \textit{inductive biases}.
Lorentz invariance, permutation invariance of jet constituents, and translational equivariance in $(\eta, \phi)$ are all symmetries that can be imposed architecturally, which tends to improve both sample efficiency and generalization.
The concept of encoding physics into network design is a recurring theme in this thesis.

Together, these properties make HEP one of the most computationally well-posed domains for machine learning.
The chapters that follow develop this observation in detail, covering supervised jet tagging (Chapter~\ref{ch:tagging}), generative and density-ratio-based unfolding (Chapters~\ref{ch:unfolding}--\ref{ch:zjets}), and the architectural building blocks underlying both (the remainder of this chapter).
